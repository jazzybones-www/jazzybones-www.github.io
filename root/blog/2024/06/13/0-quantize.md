# the jazzybones word: quantization

i'd really like to do something like a "word of the day" or "word of the month",
but i know that i'm way too lazy to write those articles on a consistent
schedule, so you're just getting a word every once in a while.

the mathematical definition of quantization is "the process of mapping an
continuous set of values to a discrete set". i hate these sorts of definitions,
so we're just going to go through some examples, each one worse than the last.

i get a grade in each of my classes every semester. there are an infinite number
of possible grades to get. i might get a 96.1829 in my calculus class while my
friend gets a 96.1830. that doesn't really matter though, because at the end of
the day we both *really* get an A+. we've quantized this infinite, continuous
set of possible grades to a finite, discrete set of letter grades.

we quantize tons of things. when a musician performs, they might be a couple of
milliseconds away from exactly on tempo, but someone listening to or
transcribing their performance would obviously be able to tell "this note was
supposed to be exactly on the downbeat". we could say that we quantize rhythms
from the infinite variety that comes from a natural performance to an exact,
discrete, mathematical point within a beat.

that quantization is baked into pretty much all sheet music since the beginning
of time. that quantization within sheet music expanded into quantization within
music software, which [J Dilla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J_Dilla) famously
misused to make "drunk" sounding music by quantizing to odd intervals such as
sevenths or elevenths of a beat.

we also quantize ourselves. every single one of us is the sum of millions of
people procreating over thousands of years across entire continents. cultures
formed and interacted and died and procreated and formed offspring who started
the entire cycle again for millenia to eventually create me, but when i was
applying to college i had to reduce all of that down to a little box that said
"East-Asian".

similarly, our gender identities are a huge part of our own self-expression and
perception. even cishet people of the same gender express themselves in
massively varied ways. you cannot convince me in a million years that the
masculine trait of "breadwinner who takes care of a family" is remotely similar
to the masculine trait of "guy who drives a big gas car", or that the feminine
trait of "homemaker" is inherently tied to the feminine trait of "person who can
give birth".

that's the problem with gender, and really all quantization in general: it traps
us in a box. in many cases, such as with GPA and musical rhythm, it doesn't
matter too much, but when we try to shove people into these quantized boxes of
"male" or "female", we lose much of the nuance that formed the boxes in the
first place. quantization is a great tool to simplify the complexity of the
world, but we need to keep in mind that it's still just a tool. if the way that
we quantize doesn't match up with the real world, it may be time to rethink
things.
