# the jazzybones word: reserves

in the united states before the great recession of 2008, we used a "limited
reserves" model.

basically, a bank offers two services: deposits and loans. let's start with
deposits. if i have $1000, i could keep it in a big wad of cash under my
mattress, or i could give it to the bank and have them hold on to it for me. at
any point, i can ask the bank for my money back, and they'll give it to me.

let's look at this from the perspective of the bank. i, the owner of jb bank,
have 10 different customers, each of which have deposited $1000. i now have
$10000. i know my customers are reasonable people, it's very unlikely that all
of my customers would want to withdraw all of their money all at once. it's so
unlikely that i'm even willing to spend $8000 of my customers' money, since they
weren't going to withdraw it anyways.

in this case, we would say that i have $8000 in excess reserves. banks take
their excess reserves and loan them out to other customers. let's say that alice
is a baker trying to start their own bakery. to do that, they have to buy the
land that the bakery sits on and the ovens used to make the food. let's say that
this would cost $8000 to buy altogether. luckily, the bank just happens to have
$8000. alice goes to the bank and says "i'll take that $8000 and use it to start
my bakery. once i have the bakery, i'll be making a lot of money, and i'll give
you back $8800 within a year." in this ideal situation, everybody wins. alice
gets to start their bakery, the bank has just made $800, and the customers never
withdrew their money so they weren't affected by this whole exchange.

this system could go wrong in a lot of places. banks are encouraged to give out
as many loans as they can. if they loan out a bunch of money at the same time
that a bunch of customers want to withdraw their money, they might find that
they don't have enough money to cover for everybody. this whole system requires
a lot of regulation. banks are required by law not to loan out a certain
percentage of their money. this requirement is called the "reserve ratio", and
the money that the bank is forced to hold is called "required reserves". the us
federal government also insures up to $250000 per person per account through the
[federal deposit insurance
corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Deposit_Insurance_Corporation),
or fdic.

many of these regulations came about after the great depression. throughout the
1920s, banks made a bunch of risky loans and used up all of their reserves, so
when the economy experienced a slight downturn and everybody withdrew all of
their money as quickly as possible, many banks failed, leaving a lot of people
penniless.

before 2008, the reserve requirement was 3%. today, it's 0%.

this model, where banks aggressively loan out as much as they can within the
confines of the law is called a "limited reserves" model, since banks try to get
rid of their cash reserves as fast as possible. in 2008, though, we switched to
an "ample reserves" model.

the federal government is seen as a completely safe investment. like, they
literally make money. if they can't pay you back, they can literally just print
more money. banks can "loan" money to the federal government for a certain
interest rate, called the [interest on
reserves](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/reserve-balances.htm).
at the time of writing, that interest rate is 5.4%. if i'm a bank, i could
choose to loan my money out to some random baker who might not be able to pay me
back at a 3% interest rate, or i could loan my money to the federal reserve at a
guaranteed 5.4% interest rate.

> sidenote: you're not really "loaning" money to the federal government, they
> kind of just pay you to keep your money. you can spend the money that you
> "loan" them at any point, so it's more of a reward for being financially
> responsible than a loan.

interest on reserves encourages banks to _not_ make aggressive loans, which
makes the banking system a bit safer. i think that this is kind of a neat
system; it encourages banks to regulate themselves without a legal requirement
for them to do so.

this system also probably has some bad implications on the economy. the biggest
one i can think of (this is purely my own personal opinions, i'm not an
economist) is that it rewards banks for doing nothing, which makes rich
bankowners get richer at the taxpayers' expense. there are probably a bunch of
other drawbacks and benefits from this system, but i'm not an economist, so i
really don't know. this kind of just feels like an engineered solution to an
economic problem, and i think that's kind of neat.
