# mathematicians and computers

i imagine that around the 1960s as computers were becoming cheaper and computer
science was developing into the field that we know it as today, the general vibe
for most people was that mathematicians' jobs would be the first to go. after
all, a mathematician's job is literally to do math, and computers can do math
way faster than any human can. today, though, computers are literally billions
of times faster than they were back then, but we still have mathematicians. so
why haven't computers replaced their jobs?

it's because math is actually really hard, at least for a computer. sure, we
have an exact process to, for example, multiply two numbers, but a
mathematician's job isn't to just multiply numbers forever, it's to discover the
process to multiply numbers in the first place, and to specifically define what
multiplication actually is. once you've done that, you can plug it all into a
computer and have it check your work, but finding that original proof is the
sort of thing that humans are really good at.

sometimes we can offload a bit of work to the computer, the [four color
problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four_color_theorem) was solved when
mathematicians said "if we can show that these 1834 really simple problems, we
can solve the entire problem, and we can develop a system to solve these really
simple problems". nobody wants to solve the same problem 1834 times, so they put
them all into a computer and had it solve them. this happens all of the time,
but we've always needed a human to actually do the complex stuff and to write
all of the code.

this is because math is a very creative process. you have these rules, and
you're trying to figure out all of the stuff that you can do within those rules
that doesn't technically break them. that's the sort of thing that humans are
really good at, and that computers are really bad at.
