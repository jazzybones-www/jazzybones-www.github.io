# wasd, hjkl, and npfb

in 2024, we have these four great buttons on the bottom right of our keyboards
called the "arrow keys". you can use them to move around in a document without
having to move to the mouse and manually click on the place that you're trying
to edit. they're really useful, and an s-tier addition to the modern keyboard.

there are other ways to navigate with the keyboard, of course. gamers will
pretty much always use the w, a, s, and d keys to move around in games. on a
standard qwerty keyboard, these keys are approximately the same shape as the
arrow keys, and they allow you to use the mouse with the right hand as you move
with the left.

there used to be a time when most keyboards didn't have arrow keys. this was
back when computers took up entire rooms and could really only be found at
universities and large corporations. people still had to navigate text with just
a keyboard, though, so in 1976 two independent systems formed to operate two
independent text editors.

[emacs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emacs), a text editor created by hackers
at mit, chose the simplest solution. they said "n" takes you to the next line,
"p" takes you to the previous line, "f" brings you forwards, and "b" brings you
backwards. n is down, p is up, f is right, b is left.

[vi](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vi_(text_editor)>), a text editor written by
Bill Joy across the country at uc berkeley, on the other hand, chose a much more
ergonomic approach. Joy looked at his keyboard and said "the hjkl keys are right
next to each other, and my hands are already in that general area anyways, let's
just use those." thus, h is left, j is down, k is up, and l is right.

so now we have four separate systems for navigating orthogonally with the
keyboard, each developed independently to fit four very similar but slightly
different environments. vi focuses on ergonomics, emacs on mathematical purity,
the arrow keys on an intuitive design that changes the hardware itself, and wasd
on a mix of intuition and ergonomics.

i think it's really neat that even something that seems really intuitive and
universal like arrow keys can be designed in so many different, perfectly valid
ways, it just shows how diverse the human experience is, and how you can't make
any assumptions about "universal" designins or ideas.
