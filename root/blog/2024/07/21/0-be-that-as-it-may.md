# the jazzybones word: be that as it may

Shakespeare is hard to study. the way that english was spoken then and the way
that it's spoken now feel more like two separate languages with a lot of shared
vocabulary than a single language with some chronological differences. like,
here are some lines from the "to be or not to be" speech that no english speaker
today would ever write unprompted.

>     When he himself might his quietus make
>     With a bare bodkin? Who would fardels bear,

Shakespearean writing is filled with archaic words and odd expressions, so
whenever i read his work i have to mentally translate into modern english. it's
like there's a layer of fog in my brain as i'm reading that i have to squint
through to find some semblance of an obscured hidden meaning. to me, reading
Shakespeare feels more like a mental exercise than an enjoyable activity.

occasionally, though, i find some phrase in perfectly modern english like "be
that as it may" that just feels so Shakespearean. it's like 90% of the writing
conventions from his time went away, but this one phrase remains.

> side note: i don't actually know if Shakespeare used this phrase, it just
> feels like he did. whether or not he actually _did_ use it is irrelevant to
> the point of this article.

Shakespeare's writing felt just as natural to Shakespeare as "be that as it may"
feels to us, which just feels weird to me. it's also weird that this specific
phrase feels natural, even though i would never use it in writing if it wasn't
already a well-known thing. like, language is just constantly changing so much,
and there's really nothing that anybody can do about it.

sometimes when i read something, i think "would someone from X years in the
past/future understand what this means?" or the slight variation, "if i were to
explain this to someone from X years in the past/future, how much would i have
to say?" there's this balance between relevancy and universality that every
writer has to hit. Shakespeare's writings aren't even completely universal,
Hamlet gets attacked by pirates because, and this is a direct quote from my high
school English teacher, "people just liked pirates back then". 

to a certain extent, it's impossible to create a fully universal textual work.
anything that you write down will slowly become harder and harder to understand
until it becomes just as foggy to future people as Shakespeare feels to me. at
that point you either have to abandon the work or actively work to understand it
at the level that contemporary readers did.

idk maybe i'm just bad at reading Shakespeare.
